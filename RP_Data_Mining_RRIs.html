A Data Mining Approach for Determining Biomechanical Adaptations in Runners Experiencing and Recovered from Patellofemoral Pain

Co-authors: Dr. Katherine Boyer, Dr. Joseph Hamill, Dr. Carl Jewell

This project has been presented at the following conferences:

  • 2022 North American Congress of Biomechanics. Ottawa, Canada. August 21-25, 2022.
  • 2022 World Congress of Biomechanics. Taipei, Taiwan. July 10-14, 2022.

Publication Abstract

Patellofemoral pain (PFP) is a common musculoskeletal pain disorder often experienced by young, athletic individuals, especially those who participate in running related activities. Multiple studies have investigated biomechanical adaptations in those with PFP, but these studies have typically analyzed discrete timepoints of the gait cycle such as peak values. These approaches may miss important biomechanical adaptations, however machine learning (ML) approaches such as principal component analysis (PCA) and classification models can account for potential adaptations that are missed using traditional discrete variable analysis methods.

Kinematics, kinetics, electromyography, and subjective pain data of runners currently experiencing, recovered from, and who never experienced PFP were collected at the beginning and end of a prolonged treadmill run. PCA was used to reduce time-series waveforms to a few principal components (PCs). The scores of these PCs were fed into a ML classification model and the most important features that differentiated runners with and without PFP were extracted from the models at both timepoints. Additionally, recovered runners were classified as healthy or symptomatic.

Important features at the beginning of the treadmill run included knee flexion angles, hip abduction moments, and gluteus maximus activation. Following the prolonged run, an additional nine features were required to achieve high accuracy, which included rotational angles at the hip and knee, knee flexion and abduction moments, and multiple ankle-based features. Recovered runners who experienced any pain throughout the run tended to be classified into the symptomatic group based on these features.

This unique analysis approach offers a viable method to systematically account for the highly complex nature of human movement, especially adaptations displayed in response to injury and pain. Apriori selection of discrete variables may also miss important biomechanical adaptations displayed by those with PFP. Lastly, although runners have recovered from pain, they may still display biomechanical adaptations similar to symptomatic individuals, which could put them at risk of redeveloping PFP.
