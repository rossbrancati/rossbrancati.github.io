Running injury meets machine learning

Due to the complex etiology of patellofemoral pain (PFP), the biomechanical adaptations presented by those with PFP can be highly dimensional. Other studies on patellofemoral pain have isolated specific joints and timepoints of the gait cycle, but those approaches may miss important features of running patterns that are altered in runners with PFP. Therefore, this project utilizes a systematic approach to determine features of running biomechanics that are important for classifying runners with and without PFP. We employed principal component analysis (PCA) to reduced high dimensionality of running biomechanics and fed PCA scores into an interpretable machine learning classification algorithm. Aging Muscle Studies

Understanding Fatigue in Older Adults (UFO) and the Study of Aging Gait and Energetics (SAGE) are two NIH funded R01 projects between multiple principal investigators at UMass. I am responsible for collecting, processing, and analyzing the biomechanics data associated with these projects.

One analysis I've been working on is analyzing postural control using a metric known at Time to Contact (TTC). TTC is defined as the time it would take an individuals center of pressure to reach a stability boundary (more details here). The results of our analyses show that strictly measuring the time that an individual can hold a certain balance position does not capture aging related declines in postural control. Detecting Fatigue in the Wild (Dissertation)

The main goal of my dissertation project is to detect fatigue outside of the lab space. Fatigue is defined as a decline in performance in response to a standardized activity, and being able to detect when someone is in a fatigued state has many implications such as preventing musculoskeletal injuries, optimizing athletic performance, and preventing falls in older adults.
